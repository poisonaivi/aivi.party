everyday carry

Bad luck shows up without warning, causing anything from an ignorable annoyance to actual danger. One way to combat the randomness of life is by creating certainties for yourself—habits or preparation that guarantees some safeties in the event of the unexpected. That could mean mental preparation, like memorizing the locations of exits; community dependency, like sharing keys within an inner circle; or preventative actions, like securing doors with deadbolt straps.

Another way to create certainly is through everyday carry. EDC isn’t so much as obtaining expensive tools as it is keeping track of what’s on your person and what you can do with it at any time. For example, whenever I travel, I always keep a knife, lockpicks, and a Flipper Zero on me. If I have a backpack, I’ll always have chargers, adapters, a flash drive, door shims, universal keys, and a screwdriver kit in it. Besides the electronics, most of it is relatively inexpensive. Depending on your needs, you may decide to run a different setup, but the goal is the same: to be able to solve the most problems with the least extra weight, and to be able to do it anywhere you are.

Since I started organizing my pockets, EDC has repeatedly proven itself far more practical than I originally anticipated. Quite a lot of bad luck can be averted just by being consistent. Also, being reliable for yourself in turn makes you reliable to the people around you, and I love being able to help out a friend.

For the future of my EDC, I want to build a Meshtastic node, get more GPIO functionality for my Flipper, and 3D-print some organizers for my loose tools.
